2025 US Top 5 Vet Schools’ New Programs: A Guide for DVM Applicants to Leverage Opportunities

As a DVM application consultant, I’ve long emphasized that successful DVM applications are not just about accumulating clinical hours or maintaining strong GPAs—they’re about aligning your experiences, interests, and goals with what top veterinary schools value. In 2025, a game-changing development has emerged: five elite US veterinary schools are launching new programs focused on feline and canine health, funded by Purina. These programs are not just academic additions; they are clear signals of the veterinary field’s priorities—and powerful opportunities for DVM applicants to stand out in a competitive pool.

In this guide, we’ll break down what these new programs really indicate for your application, how to interpret their focus areas (nutrition, dermatology, cognition, canine performance), and—most importantly—how to use this information to strategically plan your internships and strengthen your application. Whether you’re just starting to explore veterinary opportunities or deep in the application process, this insight will help you turn these school updates into your competitive advantage.

First: Understand the “Why” Behind These New Programs

Before diving into the specifics of each school’s program, it’s critical to recognize what these initiatives reveal—both about the veterinary industry and the priorities of top US vet schools. These programs are not random: they are driven by two key trends that will shape the future of veterinary medicine, and thus, how admissions committees evaluate applicants:

1. The Rising Demand for Specialized Companion Animal Care

Felines and canines are more than pets—they are family members, and pet owners increasingly seek specialized, evidence-based care for their companions. From tailored nutrition plans for pets with chronic conditions to advanced dermatology treatments for allergic skin diseases, and even cognitive support for aging dogs, the industry is shifting toward specialization and integrative care. Purina’s funding (a leader in pet nutrition and health research) and the schools’ focus areas confirm this: vet schools want graduates who can meet this growing demand for specialized care, not just general practitioners.

2. The Intersection of Research, Clinical Practice, and Industry Collaboration

All five programs blend education, training, and research—and all are supported by a major industry partner (Purina). This reflects a key priority for top vet schools:培养 applicants who can bridge the gap between scientific research and real-world clinical practice. Schools like Cornell and Penn Vet have long collaborated with Purina on research initiatives (such as canine genome sequencing and healthy aging programs), and these new programs expand that collaboration to directly benefit students. Admissions committees will now place even greater value on applicants who can demonstrate experience in this “translational” space—applying research to improve patient care.

In short: These programs are a roadmap for what top vet schools want in applicants. They’re telling you, “We value specialization in companion animal health, research experience, and an understanding of how nutrition, dermatology, and cognition intersect with clinical practice.” Your job? Align your internships and application materials with this roadmap.

Breakdown of the 5 Top Vet Schools’ New Programs (and What They Mean for You)

Each school’s program has a unique focus, but all tie back to the core themes of specialization, research, and integrative care. Below is a detailed breakdown of each program, what it signals about the school’s priorities, and how you can leverage it to your advantage.

1. University of Pennsylvania School of Veterinary Medicine (Penn Vet): Purina Canine Cognition and Performance Program

Program Details: This program expands Penn Vet’s Working Dog Center to focus on canine cognition (how dogs think, learn, and problem-solve) and performance—covering everything from lifesaving search and rescue dogs to family companions. It aims to advance the health and safety of people, pets, and the environment through improved understanding of canine behavior and ability.

What It Signals About Penn Vet: Penn Vet has long been a leader in working dog research, with ongoing studies on behavioral testing to identify successful service and working dogs. This new program reinforces its commitment to canine behavior, performance, and applied research. The school values applicants who are curious about the intersection of canine cognition, behavior, and clinical care—especially those with an interest in working dogs or companion animal behavior.

How to Leverage It:
      Internship Search: Seek opportunities at working dog centers (like Penn Vet’s own Working Dog Center, if possible), search and rescue dog training facilities, or animal behavior clinics. Even volunteering with organizations that train service dogs or conduct canine behavior research (such as studies on detection dogs) will align with this program’s focus.Application Prep: If you have experience with working dogs, behavior training, or canine cognition (even observing behavioral assessments), highlight it in your personal statement and experience descriptions. Emphasize how you gained insight into canine performance or cognition—for example, “I assisted in behavioral testing of puppies to identify traits suited for search and rescue work, which deepened my understanding of how canine cognition impacts working performance.”

2. Auburn University College of Veterinary Medicine: Purina Professorship in Veterinary Nutrition

Program Details: This program establishes a new professorship dedicated to veterinary nutrition, focusing on education and training across all areas of canine and feline nutrition. Its goal is to ensure current and future veterinarians can provide the highest level of comprehensive, integrative care—with nutrition as a cornerstone.

What It Signals About Auburn: Auburn is doubling down on veterinary nutrition as a core component of clinical care. The new assistant clinical professor role (tied to this professorship) will focus on teaching, research in metabolic diseases and intestinal microbiomics, and clinical nutrition consultations. The school values applicants who understand the critical role of nutrition in preventing and managing disease—not just as an afterthought, but as an integrative part of patient care.

How to Leverage It:
      Internship Search: Prioritize internships or shadowing opportunities that include nutrition consultations—such as small animal clinics with dedicated nutrition services, veterinary nutrition specialty practices, or even Purina’s own nutrition research facilities (which offer externships for DVM students). If you’re early in your journey, volunteer at animal shelters where nutrition plans are tailored to rescue animals’ needs.Application Prep: Highlight any experience you have with nutritional assessments, diet formulation, or client education on pet nutrition. For example, “I assisted in creating personalized diet plans for overweight cats and educated owners on nutrition’s role in weight management, aligning with Auburn’s focus on integrative care through nutrition.” If you have coursework in animal nutrition, mention it—especially if it covered feline/canine-specific nutrition.

3. University of Florida College of Veterinary Medicine (UF Vet): Purina Endowed Professor of Dermatology

Program Details: This program establishes an endowed professorship in dermatology, supporting diagnostic and interventional training, as well as research in veterinary dermatology and integrative nutrition. UF Vet already has faculty with extensive experience in veterinary dermatology, including research on allergic skin diseases and antibiotic resistance in equine dermatology.

What It Signals About UF Vet: UF Vet is prioritizing veterinary dermatology as a high-demand specialty, with a focus on both clinical training and research. The program’s link to integrative nutrition (connecting skin health to diet) also highlights the school’s commitment to holistic care. Admissions committees will value applicants with exposure to dermatology cases and an understanding of how nutrition impacts skin health.

How to Leverage It:
      Internship Search: Seek shadowing or internship opportunities at dermatology specialty clinics, small animal practices with a high volume of dermatology cases (e.g., allergies, skin infections), or research labs focused on veterinary dermatology. UF Vet’s dermatology clerkship and research programs (led by faculty like Rosanna Marsella) are also worth exploring for potential opportunities.Application Prep: Describe any experience you have observing or assisting with dermatology procedures—such as skin scrapings, allergy testing, or treatment plans for skin conditions. If you’ve learned about the link between nutrition and skin health (e.g., how certain diets help manage allergic dermatitis), emphasize that connection. For example, “I observed a dermatologist treat a dog with atopic dermatitis, learning how dietary modifications complemented medical interventions—aligning with UF Vet’s focus on integrative dermatology care.”

4. Colorado State University College of Veterinary Medicine (CSU Vet): Purina Nutrition Suite

Program Details: CSU Vet is opening a new Purina Nutrition Suite—a 4-room facility dedicated to nutrition education and training, located at the school’s Veterinary Health and Education Complex. The program builds on the existing Purina Young Investigator Award Program, which funds students and faculty mentors for scientific research.

What It Signals About CSU Vet: CSU Vet is committed tohands-on, facility-driven nutrition education and student research. The new Nutrition Suite provides students with dedicated space to learn about diet formulation, nutritional testing, and client education, while the Young Investigator Award highlights the school’s focus on student-led research. CSU values applicants who are proactive about gaining practical nutrition experience and have an interest in research.

How to Leverage It:
      Internship Search: Look for internships that offer hands-on nutrition training—such as working in a veterinary nutrition lab, assisting with diet formulation for animals with complex health conditions, or volunteering at facilities that use nutrition as part of rehabilitation. Purina’s externship programs (which include hands-on research and care) are also a great fit, as they align with CSU’s focus on nutrition and research collaboration.Application Prep: Highlight any practical nutrition experience—even basic tasks like measuring food portions for patients, assisting with nutritional assessments, or participating in research projects related to animal nutrition. If you’ve written a research paper or presented on nutrition (even in an undergraduate course), mention it. For example, “I assisted in a research project on equine nutrition and gut health, collecting data on feed intake and microbiome composition—experience that aligns with CSU’s focus on nutrition research and hands-on training.”

5. Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine: Purina Cornell Veterinary Nutrition Program

Program Details: This program builds on Cornell’s existing strength in nutrition, training, and clinical research, advancing nutritional management strategies for both healthy pets and those with complex health conditions. It operates alongside the existing Purina Cornell Canine Healthy Aging and Mobility Program, creating a multidisciplinary center of excellence for integrative pet health.

What It Signals About Cornell: Cornell is a leader in veterinary nutrition research, with a long history of collaboration with Purina on initiatives like the canine reference family DNA distribution center and healthy aging programs. This new program reinforces its focus on evidence-based nutrition, integrative care, and multidisciplinary research (combining nutrition with aging, mobility, and other areas). Cornell values applicants who can demonstrate a deep understanding of nutrition’s role in pet health, as well as experience with research or clinical care that bridges multiple specialties.

How to Leverage It:
      Internship Search: Target internships at specialty practices that offer integrative care (combining nutrition with other specialties like geriatrics or rehabilitation), veterinary nutrition research labs, or organizations like Purina that collaborate with Cornell on nutrition and aging research. Cornell’s own clinical nutrition service and research programs (including those focused on genomic nutrition) are ideal if you have the opportunity to participate.Application Prep: Emphasize experiences that show your ability to connect nutrition to other areas of veterinary care—such as nutrition for aging pets, or nutritional management of chronic diseases (e.g., diabetes, kidney disease). If you have research experience in nutrition or geriatric pet care, highlight it. For example, “I shadowed a veterinary nutritionist who developed diet plans for senior dogs with mobility issues, learning how nutrition supports joint health and aging—aligning with Cornell’s focus on integrative nutrition and healthy aging.”

Key Strategies for DVM Applicants: Turn Program Insights into Action

Now that you understand what each program means, let’s distill this into actionable steps for your internship search and application preparation. These strategies will help you stand out, regardless of which schools you’re applying to—because they align with the broader trends shaping veterinary education.

1. Stop “Randomly Accumulating Hours”—Focus on Specialized Experience

Gone are the days when “1000+ clinical hours” alone would impress admissions committees. These new programs make it clear: quality and specialization matter more than quantity. Instead of bouncing between generic internships, focus on experiences that align with the focus areas of your target schools (nutrition, dermatology, canine cognition/performance). For example:

- If you’re applying to Auburn or CSU: Prioritize nutrition-focused internships.

- If you’re applying to Penn Vet: Seek working dog or canine behavior opportunities.

- If you’re applying to UF Vet: Gain exposure to dermatology cases.

Even 50-100 hours of specialized experience will be more impactful than 500 hours of generic clinic work—because it shows you’ve thoughtfully aligned your goals with the school’s priorities.

2. Seek Research Experience (Even if You’re “Clinically Focused”)

All five programs blend research and clinical training, and top vet schools (especially Cornell, Penn Vet, and CSU) place high value on research experience. You don’t need to be a “research whiz”—even assisting with basic research tasks (data collection, literature reviews, observing experiments) will set you apart. For example:

- Reach out to professors at your undergraduate school who study animal nutrition, behavior, or dermatology—ask to volunteer in their labs.

- Apply for student research programs like Purina’s Young Investigator Award (at CSU) or summer research fellowships (similar to the University of Arizona’s Summer Scholars Research Program).

- If you can’t find formal research opportunities, document your own “observational research”—for example, tracking how diet changes impact a shelter animal’s health, or noting behavioral patterns in working dogs.

3. Highlight “Integrative Care” in Your Application

Every program emphasizes integrative care—connecting nutrition to dermatology, cognition to performance, or research to clinical practice. In your personal statement, experience descriptions, and letters of recommendation, make sure to highlight how your experiences reflect this holistic approach. For example:

- Instead of saying, “I fed dogs at a shelter,” say, “I assisted in creating tailored nutrition plans for shelter dogs with skin conditions, learning how diet impacts dermatological health—aligning with UF Vet’s focus on integrative dermatology and nutrition.”

- Instead of saying, “I observed a vet treat dogs,” say, “I observed a vet use behavioral training and nutritional supplements to improve the performance of service dogs, deepening my understanding of how cognition and nutrition intersect—consistent with Penn Vet’s new program.”

4. Use the Programs to Demonstrate “Fit” with Your Target Schools

One of the biggest mistakes DVM applicants make is sending generic applications to every school. These new programs give you a perfect opportunity to show that you’ve done your research and that you’re a good “fit” for the school. In your personal statement or secondary essays:

- Mention the specific program at the school and explain how your experiences and goals align with it. For example, “I’m particularly drawn to Cornell’s Purina Veterinary Nutrition Program because my experience developing diet plans for senior pets has ignited my passion for evidence-based nutritional management of complex health conditions—an area Cornell excels in.”

- If you’ve had an internship or research experience that ties directly to the program (e.g., working with a dermatologist for UF Vet), emphasize that connection. This shows the admissions committee that you’re not just applying to the school—you’re applying to their specific strengths.

5. Network with Faculty and Students at These Schools

Many of these programs are led by new or existing faculty (e.g., the Purina Professorship at Auburn, the Endowed Professor of Dermatology at UF Vet). Reach out to these faculty members via email (keep it brief and polite!) to ask about the program, or connect with current students at the school to learn more about how they’re engaging with the new initiatives. For example:

“Dear Professor [Last Name], I’m a DVM applicant with a passion for veterinary nutrition, and I was excited to learn about Auburn’s new Purina Professorship in Veterinary Nutrition. I’ve volunteered at a small animal clinic where I assisted with nutrition consultations, and I’m curious to learn more about how the new professorship will enhance nutrition education for students. Thank you for your time!”

Networking like this not only gives you valuable insight—it also helps you stand out as a proactive, engaged applicant. Some students even secure shadowing or research opportunities through these connections.

Final Thoughts: These Programs Are Your Advantage—Use Them

The 2025 launch of these five new programs is a gift to DVM applicants. They’re not just academic updates—they’re a clear roadmap for what top vet schools want. By focusing on specialized, integrative experiences (aligned with nutrition, dermatology, canine cognition), seeking research opportunities, and highlighting your fit with each school’s program, you’ll position yourself as a standout applicant.

Remember: DVM admissions committees are looking for applicants who are not just “qualified”—they’re looking for applicants who are passionate about the future of veterinary medicine. These programs show you where the field is heading—now it’s up to you to show the committees that you’re ready to lead it.

As your consultant, my best advice is to start small: Pick 1-2 focus areas (e.g., nutrition and dermatology) that align with your interests and target schools, and seek 1-2 high-quality internships or research experiences in those areas. Then, tell a cohesive story in your application about how those experiences have prepared you to thrive in programs like the ones we’ve discussed.

You’ve got this—and these new programs are here to help you succeed.
